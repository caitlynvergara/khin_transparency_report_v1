Khin.AI  /  Transparency Report

The Training Database

A living record of the curated sources used to train Khin as an educator on child online safety and youth mental health, helping families navigate the digital era.

Khin is being trained as an evidence-grounded educator, not an oracle. Every source listed here was reviewed, summarized, and categorized before being added to the model's reference corpus.

This document serves two purposes. It is part of Khin.AI's transparency report, and it is a standing invitation for researchers, clinicians, and family advocates to contribute. If you maintain peer-reviewed work, applied research, or family-facing resources in this space, please reach out.

01 / Benefits

Potential benefits youth are getting from social media and AI

1a   LGBTQ+ Youth LGBTQ+
Systematic Review

Unveiling the pride in social media: A review of LGBTQIA+ use of online social networks

Ebardo, R., & Payawal, B. O. (2024). Unveiling the pride in social media: A review of LGBTQIA+ use of online social networks. Asian CHI Symposium 2024. ACM. https://doi.org/10.1145/3704611.3704653

This systematic review of 41 publications maps why LGBTQIA+ users, who are disproportionately young adults, turn to social media: seeking information, exchanging peer support, and finding connections that are difficult to access in person. It also catalogs the platform affordances (community building, account management, content curation, multimedia sharing) that make these spaces functional substitutes for in-person community. The review is significant for Khin because it grounds the affirmative case for online spaces in evidence, rather than treating harm and benefit as a binary.

Survey Brief

LGBTQ young people of color in online spaces

The Trevor Project. (2023, July). LGBTQ young people of color in online spaces [Research brief]. The Trevor Project. thetrevorproject.org

Drawing on the 2023 U.S. National Survey on the Mental Health of LGBTQ Young People, this brief reports that LGBTQ young people who feel safe and understood in at least one online space have 20% lower odds of a past-year suicide attempt and 15% lower odds of recent anxiety. The protective effect on anxiety is even stronger among LGBTQ young people of color, who report TikTok, Discord, Instagram, and YouTube as their leading safe spaces. The brief is foundational evidence for Khin that blanket-restriction advice ignores genuinely life-protective uses of platforms, particularly for multiply marginalized youth.

1b   Asian American / Asian Youth Asian
Empirical Study

The acceptability of artificial intelligence to support university students' mental health

Lye, M., Martin, R., & Richmond, S. (2026). The acceptability of artificial intelligence to support university students' mental health: The role of Asian cultural values and social support. Computers in Human Behavior: Artificial Humans, 7, 100247. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chbah.2025.100247

In a sample of 135 Australian university students, adherence to Asian cultural values was positively associated with acceptability of AI-based mental health interventions, while perceived social support was not. The finding pushes back on a one-size-fits-all picture of AI mental health tools, suggesting that students with cultural norms around stigma and family privacy may be more open to AI as an entry point than to traditional services. For Khin, this is direct evidence that culturally responsive AI design can meet a need that institutional care has historically missed.

1c   General Youth General
National Survey

Use of generative AI for mental health advice among US adolescents and young adults

McBain, R. K., Bozick, R., Diliberti, M., Zhang, L. A., Zhang, F., Burnett, A., Kofner, A., Rader, B., Breslau, J., Stein, B. D., Mehrotra, A., Uscher-Pines, L., Cantor, J., & Yu, H. (2025). Use of generative AI for mental health advice among US adolescents and young adults. JAMA Network Open, 8(11), e2542281. https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.42281

The first nationally representative survey of U.S. youth ages 12 to 21 on this question found that 13.1% have used generative AI for mental health advice, rising to 22.2% in the 18 to 21 group, and that 92.7% of users described the advice as somewhat or very helpful. Black respondents were less likely to rate the advice as helpful, signaling clear equity gaps in current models. The study is the empirical anchor for Khin's design assumption that youth are already using AI for emotional support, and the relevant question is whether they use a model built for them.

Foundation Study

Remapping recovery: Understanding Gen Z's mental health journey

Harvey, A., O'Shaughnessy, L., Kuznetsova, A., & Fernandez, E. (2024). Remapping recovery: Understanding Gen Z's mental health journey. Made of Millions Foundation. madeofmillions.com

This nationally representative Gen Z study finds that 41% of young people turn to social media for mental health education, second only to healthcare professionals at 43%, and ahead of parents at 28%. It documents Gen Z as the generation most likely to acknowledge mental health struggles, seek help, and de-stigmatize care, while also showing that diagnosis timelines are far shorter than legacy clinical literature suggests (28 months on average vs. the cited 13 to 17 years). The study supports Khin's position that social media is already functioning as a frontline mental health educator, and that this role should be designed for, not ignored.

Peer Review

The future of mental health care: Peer-to-peer support and social media

Naslund, J. A., Aschbrenner, K. A., Marsch, L. A., & Bartels, S. J. (2016). The future of mental health care: Peer-to-peer support and social media. Epidemiology and Psychiatric Sciences, 25(1), 113–122. https://doi.org/10.1017/S2045796015001067

This influential review documents how people with serious mental illness, including youth, use social media to form social connections, blog about lived experience, and disclose mental health concerns at rates equal to or higher than peers without mental illness. The authors argue that online peer-to-peer networks reduce barriers (stigma, cost, in-person social demands) that block traditional care for marginalized and isolated individuals. Khin treats this paper as the foundational case for designing peer-aware educational responses, and for taking online peer support seriously as a clinical resource rather than dismissing it as distraction.

Intervention

Developing social media-based suicide prevention messages in partnership with young people

Robinson, J., Bailey, E., Hetrick, S., Paix, S., O'Donnell, M., Cox, G., Ftanou, M., & Skehan, J. (2017). Developing social media-based suicide prevention messages in partnership with young people: Exploratory study. JMIR Mental Health, 4(4), e40. https://doi.org/10.2196/mental.7847

In a six to eight week psychoeducation program, this study tested whether co-designed curricula could help young people discuss self-harm and suicide with peers more safely. Most participants reported feeling more confident having these conversations. The paper is one of the strongest pieces of evidence Khin uses to support the position that peer-to-peer disclosure can be made safer through structured education, although the authors note that the curriculum design and methodology were underspecified and warrant replication.

02 / Mental Health Problems

Mental health problems that extend from social media or AI

Longitudinal

The development of post-traumatic stress symptoms among adolescents who experience cyber and traditional victimization over time

Holfeld, B., & Mishna, F. (2021). Journal of Youth and Adolescence, 50(10), 2339–2350. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10964-021-01394-3

Following 510 Canadian adolescents (grades 7 and 10) across three academic years, this longitudinal study shows that concurrent cyber and traditional bullying victimization is uniquely associated with PTSD symptoms, and that elevated PTSD symptoms in turn predict more victimization over time, creating a bidirectional cycle. The findings legitimize cyberbullying as a trauma exposure, not merely a stressor. Khin uses this to frame conversations with families: PTSD-grade symptoms after digital harassment are clinically expected, not an overreaction.

Systematic Review

The mental health and social implications of nonconsensual sharing of intimate images on youth

Schmidt, F., Varese, F., Larkin, A., & Bucci, S. (2024). Trauma, Violence, & Abuse, 25(3), 2158–2172. https://doi.org/10.1177/15248380231207896

This systematic review of 13 eligible studies finds consistent associations between nonconsensual sharing of sexual images (NCSSI) and increased depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation among youth, compounded by bullying, harassment, and victim-blaming in the social aftermath. The authors call out the field's reliance on unvalidated measurement tools and the conflation of consensual and nonconsensual sharing. For Khin, this clarifies the distinction families often miss: the harm is the loss of consent and the social fallout, not the original image.

Focus Group

Online safety when considering self-harm and suicide-related content

La Sala, L., Sabo, A., Michail, M., Thorn, P., Lamblin, M., Browne, V., & Robinson, J. (2025). Journal of Medical Internet Research, 27, e66321. https://doi.org/10.2196/66321

Six focus groups with Australian youth (12 to 25), policy makers, and global social media industry professionals surface where these three stakeholder groups agree and disagree on online safety for self-harm and suicide content. Up to 83% of young people report having seen this content on social media, often unsearched-for, and often before age 13. The study is significant for Khin because it captures young people's own framing of where platforms, governments, and families should draw responsibility lines, input that adult-authored policy frequently omits.

Qualitative

Technology assisted child sexual abuse in the UK: Young people's views on the impact of online sexual abuse

Hamilton-Giachritsis, C., Hanson, E., Whittle, H., Alves-Costa, F., & Beech, A. (2020). Children and Youth Services Review, 119, 105451. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.childyouth.2020.105451

Drawing on the perspectives of young people who experienced technology-assisted child sexual abuse, this UK study finds that the mental health outcomes following online CSA are largely equivalent to those following offline CSA. This finding is critical because it counters a still-common belief among parents, providers, and law enforcement that online abuse is somehow less serious. Khin uses it to make sure CSAM, sextortion, and online grooming are treated with the same clinical weight as contact abuse.

03 / Research on Problem Types

Research on types of problems

Thorn / Survey

Responding to online threats: Minors' perspectives on disclosing, reporting, and blocking in 2021

Thorn. (2023). Responding to online threats: Minors' perspectives on disclosing, reporting, and blocking in 2021 [Report]. Thorn. thorn.org

Survey of 1,000 minors aged 9 to 17 finds that minors experience online sexual interactions with peers and perceived adults at concerningly similar rates, prefer online safety tools (blocking, reporting) over offline disclosure to caregivers by a 2.5x margin, and continue to face recontacts from problematic users even after blocking. The report is one of Khin's primary references on the gap between platform tools and durable safety. It also directly informs how Khin coaches families: kids are using the tools, but the tools are not closing the loop.

Thorn / Emerging

Commodified sexual interactions involving minors

Thorn. (2025, April). Commodified sexual interactions involving minors: Evolving dynamics in technology-facilitated child sexual exploitation [Report]. Thorn. thorn.org

This report introduces a new category, commodified sexual interactions, for the exchange of items of value (monetary or otherwise) for sexual interaction with a minor without a third-party facilitator, and documents how youth themselves are encountering these dynamics in their digital lives. It expands the operational definition of CSEC beyond trafficked or third-party-mediated cases. Khin uses this work to keep its understanding of CSAM, sextortion, and CSEC current rather than relying on the pre-platform terminology that still dominates parent-facing content.

Thorn / Field

Advancing response to sadistic online exploitation in networked youth environments

Thorn. (2026, May). Advancing response to sadistic online exploitation in networked youth environments [Report]. Thorn. thorn.org

Drawing on field interviews with more than 30 practitioners across eight countries, this report defines sadistic online exploitation (SOE) as youth-driven, networked environments where harm itself becomes the basis for status and belonging, with adolescents acting as both victims and participants. It documents how existing single-domain classification frameworks fail to capture the multi-modal nature of SOE. For Khin, this is essential context that the most dangerous emerging environments are not adult predator to child, but peer-network ecosystems with normalized escalation.

Stanford Workshop

Stanford youth safety and digital wellbeing report, 2025

Linos, E., Hancock, J., Lopez, M. A., Harrison, V., Lee, A. Y., Stevic, A., Liu, S., Lembke, A., Katz, R., & Jin, K.-X. (2025). Stanford youth safety and digital wellbeing report, 2025. Stanford CDH & SML. cdh.stanford.edu

This Stanford workshop report introduces the Integrated Harm Framework (IHF), an expert-rated taxonomy of 22 specific youth-specific social media harms across four conceptual categories (safety/criminal, health/wellbeing, content-driven, other). The top-rated harms include adult-minor solicitation, CSAM/CSEM, self-harm/suicide-promoting communities, and bullying/harassment. Khin uses the IHF as its working taxonomy when distinguishing between harms families ask about, so the model can be precise about scale and mitigation rather than collapsing distinct risks into a single screen-time conversation.

Framework

A framework for digital safety: Designing social media interventions

Vergara, C., Ranka, H., Kothari, N., Lalwani, J., & Mehta, S. (2024). A framework for digital safety: Designing social media interventions [Working paper]. SimPPL. ssrn.com

This paper proposes a four-parameter taxonomy (focus, scope, driver, user journey) for classifying social media interventions, applied to TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube. It distinguishes between platform features (neutral functionality) and interventions (intentional harm-reduction measures), and provides a common language for cross-platform comparison. Khin's recommendation logic for families, including when to nudge a setting change vs. when to escalate, is built on this taxonomy, and the framework is a self-citation maintained to make Khin's reasoning auditable.

News / Context

Mentions of trans kids scrubbed from national child safety clearinghouse website

Robertson, A. (2025). Mentions of trans kids scrubbed from national child safety clearinghouse website. The Verge. theverge.com

This reported piece documents NCMEC's removal of transgender-youth references from its website following conservative-media criticism, and the resulting concern from advocates that LGBTQ+ youth lose access to safety resources designed for them. NCMEC characterized the change as a routine update. The piece is included to flag a critical political-context risk for Khin: family-facing resources upstream of the model can be quietly de-scoped, and the model needs awareness of which institutional sources have recently changed their stance on LGBTQ+ youth coverage.

04 / Resources for Families

Resources for families

Educator Guide

Suggested supplemental materials on online bullying and attacks

Vergara, C. (n.d.). Suggested supplemental materials on online bullying and attacks: Foundational skills module 3, managing risk [Educator resource]. Harvard Medical School.

A curated educator resource pointing families and educators to NCMEC's Cyberbullying article, Thorn's Sexting and Nonconsensual Sharing research, Thorn's Disclosure and Reporting findings, and NCMEC/Take It Down's online safety tools. Each entry includes a short description of the resource's audience and purpose. This is one of two front-line, family-facing references in Khin's corpus and is used to ground "what do I do next" responses in vetted external services.

Educator Guide

Understanding CSAM and sextortion: A guide for educators

Vergara, C. (n.d.). Understanding CSAM and sextortion: A guide for educators [Educator resource]. Harvard Medical School.

A one-page educator brief defining CSAM (including AI-generated content depicting real children) and sextortion, with NCMEC's three direct intake services: Team HOPE (peer support), Take It Down (image removal for under-18s), and the Family Advocacy Network (trauma-trained mental health referrals). It also documents the 6,835 to 440,419 increase in CSAM reports in the first half of 2025 and the one-in-five teen sextortion exposure rate from Thorn. Khin uses this resource to give families a fast, accurate next step the moment they describe a CSAM or sextortion situation.
